All you need to get good at something is theory, practice and time

27-03-2026

But something I have been grappling with is how should you proportion your time?

If theory without practice is empty and practice without theory is blind how do we do both without neglecting the other?

If you must neglect one, neglect the right one!

We must understand that optimising the ratio of practice and theory from the top down is a bit silly. If you do want the simplest and most actionable advice that can be preplanned you should in general lean on practice.

Focus on the desired outcome

In general I think that focusing more on practice is always the better idea because while you can learn theory without practice, you need a certain level of theory to practice

If you have looked at any criticism of standardised testing you have probably heard something akin to "Building an education system arounds tests teaches students how to get good at tests instead of the related field"

Theory and "Raw Knowledge" is definitely required to an extent but if you want to get good at tests you have to get good at taking tests. It is not enough to simply have all the information.

If your goal is to pass or excel at an exam you need to learn more then just the content of it. After learning enough raw knowledge to engage with a practice test, I think you should do practice tests more then dedicating time to purely recall or purely new information. Practice tests exercise multiple muscles at once. You practice retrieving information (instead of just using flash cards), processing that information into a required format (instead of just aimless essay writing or individual practice questions), in a timed environment.

Maybe the muscle analogy can be expanded. Like I said you have to have enough base knowledge to actually do a practice exam. So if that "muscle" is weak then you should "train" it by studying and doing an exercise focused purely on retrieval.

Boiling it down to a science

Step 1: Projects and Goals

It is my belief that learning theory increases your capacity to do things while doing practice increases the skill at doing the stuff.

This thought parlays into another one. In general you should increase your capacity in proportion with your needs.

This is why I am an advocate of making your own projects purely for the sake of learning. You have created a scenario where you must increase your capacity to do something because you need to complete some goal.

If you have a broad goal like "get good grades" you have to first break it down into what are the conditions that need to be met to achieve that goal.

To achieve good grades you have to do well on all your exams.

So each exam should be its own "project"

Step 2: Skills

Each project requires some skills. So we should have a nice list of all the skills required to achieve the goal of the project

Lets say our project is "History Exam" and the goal is to get an 80%.

So what are the skills it takes to achieve this?

What I came up with is you need to be able to:

  1. Retrieve the knowledge asked for by the rubric
  2. Retrieve specific evidence to base claims on
  3. Format information in the format desired by each potential question

Step 3: Designing Processes

Now that you have a list of skills, you should design practice exercises for each one

So the skill "Retrieve knowledge asked for by the rubric"

You design a process that practices that skill.

Heres what I came up with:

  1. Take RUBRIC POINT 1 (prioritise the one that has the most weighting towards it)
  2. Choose a dedicated retrieval practice activity such as flash cards
  3. Research the rubric point, make notes and turn them into flashcards
  4. Use the 2357 method with the flashcards
  5. Repeat for each rubric point

Step 4: Compound Processes

You do not have to complete each process you have just designed if you have reached a certian level of compotency (although if you have the time for it I think it can be valuable)

This is the value of practice exams I went on about earlier. In this scenario practice exams strengthen every skill to meet the goal of "getting a good grade for this exam".

Compound Processes(or Practices) are the best possible practice. After you have exercised each muscle enough to preform the compound exercise, you should try switching to it.

Notes:

Each process should include a reflection stage (especially compound ones). If you feel like you are doing a pushup wrong, record yourself and reflect. Similarly if you are doing practice exams you should go back, see what you got wrong and see how you can improve. Design a process for reflection.
